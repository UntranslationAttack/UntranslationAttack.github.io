When Translators Refuse to Translate: A Novel Attack to
Speech Translation Systems

Anonymous Authors

Abstract

Speech translation, which converts a spoken language into another spoken or written language, has experienced rapid advance recently. However, the security in this domain remains underexplored. In this work, we uncover a novel security threat unique to speech translation systems, which is dubbed "untranslation attack". We observe that state-of-theart (SOTA) models, despite their strong translation capabilities, exhibit an inherent tendency to output the content in the original speech language rather than the desired target language. Leveraging this phenomenon, we propose an attack model that deceives the system into outputting the original language content instead of translating it. Interestingly, we find that this approach achieves significant attack effectiveness with minimal overhead compared to traditional semantic perturbation attacks: it achieves a high attack success rate of 87.5% with a perturbation budget of as low as 0.001. Furthermore, we extend this approach to develop a universal perturbation attack, successfully testing it in the physical world.

Sample Level Attack

We present adversarial examples in a sample-level attack scenario. Audio samples were randomly selected from the Common Voice Delta Segment 17.0 English dataset and the MuST-C release 3 dataset. Using the proposed untranslation attack, we generated adversarial examples with varying perturbation budgets. These adversarial attacks were tested on state-of-the-art speech translation models, including Seamless M4T v2 Large and Seamless Expressive.

Baseline Untargeted Attack Samples

We demonstrate the examples from Table 1 of the Motivation section, showcasing adversarial audio generated by traditional untargeted attacks alongside their corresponding translation outputs. Our findings indicate that traditional attacks, which aim to simply alter the translation, generally have a limited impact on the semantic meaning of the output, thereby weakening the effectiveness of the attack. In contrast, our proposed untranslation attack does not perturb the semantics but instead misleads the model into outputting the original language, exploiting the language barrier to ensure the translation unintelligible to the user.
